# Proposal and Close Control

Use this document once an opportunity reaches the point where a proposal may be
sent or a buying decision is actively being pursued.

The objective is simple:

- send proposals only when they are earned
- keep commercial scope honest
- protect pricing discipline
- force a real decision path

## 1. Proposal gate

Do not send a proposal unless all of this is true:

- the pain is explicit
- the use case is concrete
- the pilot format has been explained
- there is a visible owner or buying path
- the next 30-45 day window is plausible
- the opportunity is already `pilot qualified`

If one of these is missing, send recap and clarification, not proposal.

## 2. What a proposal is for

A proposal is not a generic brochure.

A proposal exists to:

- define scope
- define pilot structure
- define commercial terms
- create a decision step

If it does not create a decision step, it is not doing its job.

## 3. Minimum proposal content

Every proposal should include:

- account name
- contact / buyer context
- pain summary
- pilot scope
- what is included
- timeline
- success criteria
- pilot price
- assumptions and exclusions
- next decision step

Keep the first proposal narrow.

## 4. Scope discipline

The first proposal should not try to solve everything.

Keep it limited to:

- one operating team
- one main workflow
- one guided pilot format
- one success definition

Bad proposal behavior:

- adding optional custom work too early
- widening scope to justify price
- mixing rollout dreams into the pilot structure

## 5. Pricing rule

When the proposal is sent:

- anchor to the approved pilot structure
- keep wording direct
- do not apologize for price
- do not invent discounts pre-emptively

If resistance appears, reduce scope before discount.

## 6. Discount control

Only consider discount when there is a real reason such as:

- strategic logo value
- strong case-study value
- fast-close commitment
- clearly limited early-adopter exception

Do not discount because:

- the buyer asks immediately
- the buyer compares you to cheap generic SaaS
- the proposal feels expensive without proper scope discussion

Discount is a controlled exception, not a closing habit.

## 7. Send rule

When sending the proposal:

- state what the proposal is for
- restate the pain briefly
- state the recommended next step
- propose a concrete decision review point

Good send behavior:

- concise
- commercial
- decision-oriented

Bad send behavior:

- large unfocused email
- too many attachments
- no decision date

## 8. Decision pending rule

Once the proposal is sent, the account should move to:

- `decision pending`

Only if:

- the client has received the proposal
- you know what should happen next
- a follow-up date exists

Never send a proposal and then disappear.

## 9. Follow-up cadence after proposal

Minimum control:

- Day 0: proposal sent
- Day 2-3 working days: first follow-up
- Day 5-7 working days: second follow-up or decision review
- Day 10 working days: either real movement, nurture, or disqualify review

If the client remains vague beyond this, pipeline quality is at risk.

## 10. What to ask in follow-up

Follow-up should clarify one of these:

- fit confirmed
- scope concern
- pricing concern
- timing concern
- authority gap

If you do not know which one it is, the follow-up is too weak.

## 11. Close outcomes

Every proposal should end in one of these outcomes:

1. won
2. nurture
3. disqualified / lost

`Decision pending` is a temporary stage, not an outcome.

## 12. Signs the deal is real

The deal is real when you see some of the following:

- they ask precise scope questions
- they ask implementation questions
- they involve an operational owner
- they involve a decision-maker
- they react to start timing
- they discuss pilot success criteria

Interest without operational commitment is not enough.

## 13. Signs the deal is weak

Treat these as warning signs:

- they repeatedly avoid the next meeting
- they ask for lower price without confirming scope
- they ask for “more information” without engaging specifics
- they keep the conversation abstract
- they show no buyer-side owner

Do not inflate forecast confidence around these accounts.

## 14. Forecast rule

Do not forecast from emotion.

Forecast strength should reflect:

- stage truth
- buyer behavior
- clarity of next step
- timing reality

If any of those are weak, confidence must be reduced.

## 15. Rule to remember

Proposal is not the finish line.

It is the point where commercial seriousness must become visible.
