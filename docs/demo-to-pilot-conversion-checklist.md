# Demo to Pilot Conversion Checklist

Use this checklist once an account has replied or accepted a first meeting.

The purpose is operational:

- keep momentum
- qualify hard
- avoid free consulting
- convert fit into a paid pilot decision

## 1. Before the demo

Confirm all of this before the call:

- [ ] the account is in the CRM with owner, stage, and next action
- [ ] you know who asked for the meeting
- [ ] you know whether they are agency owner, operations, recruitment lead, or legal/admin
- [ ] you have one clear pain hypothesis
- [ ] you have one relevant candidate-document workflow example
- [ ] you know the target close for the call:
  - second meeting
  - pilot scope review
  - commercial proposal

Do not run a generic demo.

## 2. What must be validated in the demo

You need evidence for these points:

- document volume exists
- manual review pain is real
- delays, mistakes, or lack of visibility are costly
- there is an actual operator or team who would use the system
- they can run a guided pilot in the next 30-45 days

If these points do not validate, do not force a proposal.

## 3. Questions that must be answered

Leave every demo with answers to:

1. What document types create the most friction today?
2. Who reviews them now?
3. How many candidates or documents are processed weekly?
4. Where do errors or delays happen most often?
5. Who approves a new tool or pilot?
6. What would make a pilot clearly successful in the first 30 days?

If 4 or more of these are unanswered, the opportunity is not ready.

## 4. Minimum success criteria for a paid pilot

Do not move to proposal without defining at least:

- one operating team
- one document workflow in scope
- one owner on the client side
- one measurable success outcome
- one target start window

Examples of valid success outcomes:

- reduce manual review time
- improve document status visibility
- reduce OCR correction workload
- centralize candidate document tracking

## 5. Red flags

Treat these as real risk signals:

- they want a broad platform tour but cannot name a concrete pain
- they want custom development before pilot validation
- they ask for pricing but avoid scope and ownership
- they cannot identify who would run the pilot internally
- they want a free trial with operational use

Three red flags usually means do not advance fast.

## 6. End of demo rule

End the call with exactly one of these outcomes:

1. qualified for pilot scope session
2. qualified for commercial proposal
3. nurture and revisit later
4. disqualify

Never end with an undefined "we will think about it".

## 7. Immediate post-demo actions

Within the same day:

- [ ] update CRM stage
- [ ] write concise notes
- [ ] record the validated pain
- [ ] record the buying role(s)
- [ ] record blockers or objections
- [ ] schedule the next step
- [ ] send the follow-up message

If the next step is not booked, the opportunity is still at risk.

## 8. When to send pricing

Send pricing only when all of this is true:

- the pain is explicit
- a real workflow is in scope
- there is a likely owner
- there is a plausible start window
- the guided pilot model has been explained

If these conditions are missing, send recap and scope first, not pricing.

## 9. Minimum proposal structure

Every pilot proposal should contain:

- client account name
- business pain summary
- pilot scope
- what is included
- implementation format
- timeline
- success criteria
- pilot price
- next decision step

Keep the first proposal narrow and commercial.

## 10. Decision rule

Advance only if the opportunity is both:

- operationally real
- commercially plausible

If only one of those is true, keep qualification open and do not pretend it is pipeline.

## 11. Definition of a good converted opportunity

A good converted opportunity is one where:

- the client has acknowledged pain
- the use case is concrete
- the owner is visible
- the next meeting is booked
- the path to a paid pilot is explicit

That is enough for a healthy next step.
